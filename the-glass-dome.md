# The Glass Dome

**Narrative Essay by Fereshteh Sheikhjani, April 2026**

This is an excerpt. Full text available upon request for juries and editors. 

<br>

> *A wartime meditation from a seventh-floor apartment in Tehran, where binoculars, windows, and the mountain's forbidden vantage point become instruments of orientation. The essay asks what it means to stand somewhere—physically, politically—when the ground is unstable, and why seeing for oneself matters more than any political abstraction.*

Excerpt
I stayed in both wars. There is no pride in this decision. No signs of courage, no patriotism, no attachment to home, no stubbornness, not even practicality. None of these motives challenge the logic of leaving, of seeking physical protection. The danger, with all its dispersed threats, has not yet reached my doorstep. It is still a few neighborhoods away, a few streets, a few buildings, a few steps. What is it that makes me stay? Is it because it matters where I stand in the war? That question is what I read between the lines of my notes.

When I travel to other cities, I automatically assume the high ground is north. That’s often not the case, and I easily get disoriented. In Tehran just find the mountains. That’s north. That’s how I know where I stand. 

On a clear day, from the northern mountains, for example in the recreational area on Mount Tochal, the entire city lies beneath, like a sea of little cubes. The defining feature of Tehran's landscape is indeed the mountains. 

On one of the first nights of the war, when I expected bombardment and antiaircraft strikes, I planned to go to the mountain recreational area. From that ideal vantage point, at a safe distance, I would watch the city as a whole under the strikes. From that height, the city’s tangled, overwhelming mass surrenders its scale. It ceases to resist being read. 

Expectedly, the place wasn’t crowded as usual. On my way up, I saw a stranger, a shady-looking man, following me. In the emptiness of this usually crowded public place, I felt unsafe and forgot about the war entirely.
 
When the man finally took a closer step toward me, I was in total panic. He then started saying something I didn't expect: "Ma'am, it's forbidden to go higher from here. You have to get back immediately!" He was part of the plainclothes forces and only wanted me to leave. His colleagues were waiting a bit further at the turn of the road. It is said that many locally controlled drone attacks are launched from mountain heights, places that are both empty and commanding.

They had denied me the closest thing to a total view. I didn't dare protest their paranoia, but I understood it. Before today’s mediated technologies of seeing, a mounted soldier would climb a hill to observe the enemy’s camp—their resources, their positions, their weaknesses. In warfare, seeing has always been crucial. Right in the middle of the war, a curious citizen like me had become a potential military observer.
