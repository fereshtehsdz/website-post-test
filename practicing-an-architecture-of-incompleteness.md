# Practicing an Architecture of Incompleteness

**Architectural essay by Fereshteh Assadzadeh Sheikhjani, 2025** 

The essay appeared in the book [The Roofless Truth](https://www.park-books.com/en/product/the-roofless-truth/1725) edited by Matthias Drilling and Fabian Neuhaus.

### *An account of an experimental architectural studio in Tehran's southern neighborhoods, where students learn to suspend their instinct to "fix" and instead observe what already exists: fragment-walls, scorched surfaces, Iranit panels, drug dealers' escape routes, and a grandmotherly economy that wastes nothing. The essay argues that incompleteness (nakameli) is not a flaw but a resource—and that architecture's role might be to work alongside it, not erase it.*

From 2023 to the time of writing, I’ve worked with a group—mostly young people with architectural backgrounds—on a project that placed them in direct, sustained, and experimental engagement with neighborhoods in the south side of Tehran, particularly Khazaneh and Naziabad. They were invited to reflect creatively on spatial questions and generate architectural projects. These were conceived as disciplinary experiments rather than direct interventions. Still, the context—and their growing understanding of it—played a decisive role in shaping the work.

This unfolded across two editions of a six-month educational studio, guided by an intellectual framework we call Nakameli (Incompleteness)—a term resonant with the lived realities of these neighborhoods. In Iran reliance on standardized methods, planning, technologies, and materials often reinforces external dependency and reproduces poverty. Nakameli seeks to embrace the flaws embedded in difficult contexts, uncovering overlooked resources and putting them to creative use.

<br>

## The North and the South
The unequal distribution of resources has inscribed social inequalities onto Tehran’s topography. As a result, the higher foothill areas in the north benefit from a milder climate, more abundant water, and greener landscapes. Once summer retreats, these neighborhoods now house the urban elite in low-density areas with ample amenities. 
 
As we move south, the air grows noticeably warmer and drier. Water sources from the mountains become scarcer, and wind patterns tend to funnel pollution into these lower zones. Historically designated for industry and transport, southern Tehran became residential as the city expanded dramatically over the past century. The concentration of dense populations into small housing units with limited infrastructure has shaped an urban fabric marked by scarcity, deficits and stress, conditions that make it a challenging context.

<br>

## Close-Up
While thinking about how Studio Nakameli encountered the city’s south side, I found myself thinking of Abbas Kiarostami’s Close-Up, a film that profoundly shaped my view of authorship. In it, Kiarostami does not merely observe events from a distance; he enters the scene, reenacts moments, and even stages encounters and makes editorial decisions that influence the unfolding of the story. Through this interplay of proximity and construction, a kind of truth emerges—not in spite of mediation, but through it.

I have followed a similar logic in this writing. As an instructor, I was present, helping shape the intellectual, experimental, and logistical direction of the studio. Alongside the director and others, I influenced the way the studio approached its work. At the same time, I maintained a position of attentive distance: I took notes, reflected, and engaged in conversations about what was taking place. I was embedded in the environment I was studying, often participating in shaping it. Writing has become a space in which to recount, interpret, and reframe that experience, through a lens that is personal yet grounded, observational yet involved, and uninterested in offering generalized conclusions or final resolutions. 

<br>

## Where a Room Once Was
On a wall along a street in the Naziabad neighborhood, the imprint of a brick column is still visible. Rectangular recesses hint at cupboards or niches, and beneath layers of old paint, a shard of mirror survives. Part of the wall is tiled with floral patterns, pointing to a past interior now exposed to the street.
Weather has worn it down. Rainwater has left streaks, and peeling paint reveals uneven surfaces. Faded graffiti remain, including one legible word: sex. Garbage collectors lean sacks of recyclables against it before moving on. Scorch marks, likely from fires lit by the unhoused, and remnants of food and clothing linger. This fragment-wall offers shelter to those the city has cast out.

Behind it rises a large housing complex of red brick facades and tall plane trees: the Chahar-Sad Dastgah project, built about eighty years ago for industrial workers. Small units with standard amenities were set among generous public green spaces. Some attribute its design to a former secretary of the Tudeh Party, arguing that the housing typologies supported working-class movements culminating in the 1979 Revolution.

## Beautification Projects
In 2019, Tehran’s “Beautification” Organization marked around fifteen small vacant lots—each about 50 square meters—in the city’s southern neighborhoods. Each was handed to a prominent architect to be reimagined as a small public space. I was assigned one of these plots on behalf of a participating architect. The wall I’ve described stands as part of it. 

The head of the Beautification Organization seemed to hope this initiative would serve as a political springboard. In meetings, I saw renderings by other architects. One showed a striking white structure set against older buildings. It seemed to erase whatever existed on the small plot and to push the dense urban fabric outward and occupy the empty space. My attention caught on the human figures in the rendering, cut-out photos of real children: street kids or child laborers in mismatched clothes and worn plastic sandals. They stood out starkly against the gleaming white surface, staring straight into the camera.

Was it meant to suggest these children had accepted the architecture as their own?
Soon after, protests broke out. In late 2019, following a sudden spike in fuel prices, demonstrations began in low-income neighborhoods and quickly spread nationwide. The unrest went far beyond the issue of gasoline. In such a volatile climate, the Beautification Organization quietly realized that any state-led project risked rejection. The initiative was suspended—unofficially, but almost certainly for good.

<br>

## Neglect Runs Deep
The fragment-wall that had become a shelter in Naziabad is not unique in South Tehran. But Naziabad is something like the “north of the south”—its urban fabric is relatively better maintained. In many other neighborhoods neglect runs deeper. The fragment-walls, spread all over south of Tehran, resemble scattered puzzle pieces that no longer fit together, but still, side by side, they form a kind of collective shelter, for all those without a home.

My search for these walls continued and in the Oudlajan neighborhood, I came across a large vacant lot ringed by fragment-walls—remnants of homes long since demolished. In recent years, Oudlajan’s historic fabric has drawn renewed attention. A few buildings have been restored, but most interventions are superficial, aimed at cosmetics. Once one of five neighborhoods inside Tehran’s old city walls, Oudlajan was historically home to a significant Jewish population. Today, Afghan migrant families live among warehouses and workshops in a deteriorating, insecure fabric with little access to public infrastructure.

Tehran’s Grand Bazaar—still the economic core of the city—borders the neighborhood and depends on Oudlajan’s empty spaces and precarious population for storage and low-wage labor. Some of the remaining walls still bear decorative elements one could call historic—faint traces of the neighborhood’s affluent past. A conservation expert might view them with “regret.” 

A famous street artist has picked this perfect location as a site of “ruin porn:” as children are playing and addicts are injecting heroin, one notices these simple crimson figures on ruinous walls drained in history, drawings that actually resemble cave paintings from humanity’s earliest shelters. 

But the walls I find more telling are the ordinary ones: the remnants of modest homes from more recent decades, marked by plain, practical details. These are not seen as culturally valuable—unlike the “historic” ruins—but they speak to everyday lives on the threshold of visibility. I doubt the street artist would pick my walls for his art.

## Where Two Edges Meet
In search of more ordinary fragment-walls, I returned east of Naziabad, to Khazaneh. Locals call this area Lab-e Khat—“edge of the line”—because it borders the above-ground railway and metro tracks. To secure the rail corridor, a tall, thick concrete wall has been built, slicing through the neighborhood and dividing it in two. Its perspective is stark and forceful, a long, uninterrupted line—an almost impenetrable monolith born of infrastructural needs and city-scale masterplans.

Facing this massive barrier, countless small fragment-walls remain from demolished houses—homes torn down because they stood too close to the tracks. Along the “edge of the line,” these remnants and the neighboring houses shelter those living at society’s margins: the unhoused, or families in cramped, semi-formal dwellings without basic amenities. 

The space between the monolithic wall and the fragment-walls feels charged, like the gap between two identical poles of a magnet. They appear to stand in opposition, yet they are two faces of the same coin, existing because of each other. 

This liminal strip becomes a charged threshold between those whose decisions and narratives shape the city and those who live with their consequences. As I stood there, a faint question started to linger: what kind of relationship might this tension—the interstice between walls—forge with the way people inhabit space here, and with the spatial questions the place provokes?

## Grandmotherly Approach
Mohamadreza Ghodousi, my collaborator and the director of Studio Nakameli, also leads ZAV Architects and coined the term Nakameli.

Reza often speaks of what he calls the grandmotherly approach. In arid parts of Iran, scarcity has long shaped life. Over generations, people developed ways of living within limits. Grandmothers managed large households with minimal resources: the best fruits were eaten fresh, bruised ones became jam, and even the pits were turned into snacks. In this economy of care, nothing was wasted: “what is cast aside today might find a purpose tomorrow”.

This ethos is perhaps best embodied in the Persian carpet. Its raw material sheep’s wool is inexpensive and abundant. Natural waste like pomegranate skins or walnut husks is used to dye the yarn, which is then woven, knot by knot, into intricate patterns that transform humble elements into objects of immense value. These place-based techniques, perfectly attuned to what the environment could offer, made survival possible while generating beauty and value.

The rise of the oil economy brought sudden wealth, but it was spent largely on imported materials, technologies, and ideas. Many of the labor-intensive, place-based practices fell away. Oil wealth favored ready-made, standardized, and predictable solutions—masterplans designed to “fix” and control the unruliness of difficult places, relying on imported materials and engineering systems that consumed more resources than they generated. Layered, site-specific solutions began to seem ambiguous, inefficient, even flawed.

<br>

## Learning from the Ingenuity of Incompleteness
Architecture followed the same trajectory. Instead of beginning from the here and now with all its imperfections and nakamelis we imported idealized models from elsewhere, attempting to replicate them with materials and methods unsuited to a different set of conditions. The duality Reza highlights contrasts oil-fueled top-down order with ground-level resourcefulness rooted in scarcity, inspiring the concept of Studio Nakameli. 

In Persian, kāmel (کامل) means complete, whole, coherent – something that would fall apart if you tried to subtract from it, add to it, or alter its order. Adding -i (ی) forms a new word: kāmeli (کاملی), an uncommon term referring to the state of being complete. The prefix nā- (نا-) negates it, forming nākāmeli (ناکاملی) – a word even more unusual in sound – incompletion, flaw, or disorder, marked by lack, excess, or disrupted hierarchies.

The question for us at Studio Nakameli becomes: how can a discipline grounded in control, categorisation, and coherence learn to work alongside the ingenuity of incompleteness without erasing it?

In a long mezzanine, four large tables sit close together. Around each, students from one of four studios— one of them being Nakameli—develop projects aligned with their studio’s focus. Students are placed based on skills and approaches, following an initial selection.

These studios are part of a six-month program by the Contemporary Architects Association of Iran, directed by architect Alireza Taghaboni. It offers an alternative to the country’s university system, often seen as disconnected from younger generations. Though it grants no degrees, its popularity has steadily grown. Each studio works independently but regularly gathers to exchange ideas. During joint juries, all projects are reviewed side by side. It’s a collective learning process – for students, instructors, and even directors.

At the Nakameli table, Reza sits with the students and Mahshid, another instructor, lies still under a leopard-print blanket. The students are asked to describe only what they see. Forget that this is Mahshid. Forget the blanket. Forget “leopard print.” This is the studio’s opening exercise.

## What Pricks Me (Punctum)
We asked the students to meet us at a pedestrian bridge in Lab-e Khat. We walk with them through the nighborhood of Khazaneh, asking them to observe what draws their attention.

Reza has already spoken about Barthes’ concepts of studium and punctum studium being shared cultural meaning; punctum the personal detail that pierces perception. He urges students to suspend what they know, to notice what truly catches their gaze. But still, they begin as outsiders.
Khazaneh, in the south of Tehran, is a mix of formal and informal urban fabric. It has long been home to internal migrants working in nearby industrial sites. The railway divides Khazaneh and creates ruptures: dead-ends, empty lots, spatial breaks. These have contributed to its vulnerability.

Most students come from middle-class backgrounds, and neighborhoods like Khazaneh are unfamiliar. The tangled dead-ends make them uneasy. The forms of creativity shaped by scarcity and by a looser presence of planning institutions seem hard to relate to. They’re struck by the absence of order, by things out of place, by a sense of improvisation. Some notice signs of self-regulation, small systems and adaptations that hold things together. They’re intrigued by the sense of community and support. There’s curiosity about the inner life of homes and what remains hidden. The density of life in small domestic spaces catches their attention. At first, their gaze is marked by distance, a kind of exoticism.

<br>

## From Visitors to Participants
When the students think about design, their instinct is to bring order, to improve, to fix. But returning again and again, they begin to blend in. They make friends, enter homes, linger in public spaces. They shift from puzzled visitors to participant observers. They are no longer looking for solutions. They begin to realize that top-down plans often move forward without asking questions. That what first appeared as flaws—nakamelis—are not always problems to be fixed, but openings. Gaps in the dominant order that might allow something else to take root—even if imperfect. 

Students are encouraged to stay with ambiguity, to delay conclusions. They now seek a question, not an answer—one that emerges from the place itself. As they refine the lens through punctum-oriented seeing, they now begin to look for their own ways of ordering the world, ways that grow from conditions on the ground, in order to develop a design language rooted in experience. What follows is the account of some of these experiences.

## Scorches on Walls
Daghan in Persian means both “wrecked” and “scorches.” Students were drawn to the fragment-walls—examining cracks, traces, textures, and colors: natural or human, sudden or gradual. Their focus soon widened. Many of these walls were the remains of houses demolished for being within the railway buffer zone. Often, only part of a structure fell inside the boundary, yet entire homes were torn down—leaving jagged edges pressed against the straight line of a new concrete wall.

What began as a formal and tectonic study, shifted once they started speaking with residents. They met older locals and sought out those displaced by demolition. One neighbor, a retired factory technician, sketched the three houses that had once stood beside his own. The drawing wavered between plan and perspective, 2D and 3D, its flaws becoming a distinctive representational tool, a way to channel local memory into reimagining an erased past.

The students learned about the occupations of former neighbors: a blacksmith, a truck driver, a carpet cleaner and studied their tools and workshops. They noticed each profession possessed instruments with destructive potential, the blacksmith’s hammers, the cleaner’s acids and solvents, the driver’s truck.

This led to a speculative proposal: a negotiated demolition in which residents and the municipality agree to dismantle only the parts of houses that fall within the railway buffer. Neighbors themselves would do the work, using tools from their own professions. The remaining structures would be incorporated into existing homes and adapted to new uses as residents saw fit.

<br>

## Benign Criminals
At first, the students approached the neighborhood cautiously. Its maze of dead-end alleys felt unsafe, especially to three young women. Intrigued by what lay behind the high walls, they peered through every gap. One day, they noticed a street-level drug dealer covering an impossible distance in seconds. That’s when they realised: hidden passageways threaded the neighborhood behind those walls.

They booked appointments at a local salon (in Iran, salons are typically gender-specific), befriended women, and were invited into their homes. From the rooftops, they discovered a secret network: roofs linked together to enable discreet movement across the neighborhood. In a place where much of the economy revolved around drug trafficking, this network was a vital part of daily life.

Many of these rooftops and walls were built from Iranit (corrugated cement panels), supplied and worked by a migrant family from northwestern Iran, the Niyaraghis. Lightweight, cheap, and noisy underfoot, Iranit was perfect for makeshift walkways and barriers, doubling as a built-in alarm system.
During one visit, a student recalled a rap verse:

<br>

*“Why are you killing the dealers on the corner?
What are you doing about the real criminals?
Let’s face the root of the problem.
The reasons behind addiction burn down more homes than the addicts themselves.
Should an addict belong in prison?
And by the way, the reason I’m writing this verse is not that I use drugs.”*

<br>

It reframed their approach. What could they design that, like Iranit, truly served the place? They called the project Biyaraghi—a play on the family name Niyaraghi.

They began noticing other improvised structures: reused wooden beehive boxes, and a stripped-down metal frame offering stability with minimal material. These small, inventive acts, “Khazaneh creativity”, became their inspiration.

They designed a modular structure combining these elements with their own architectural language. It could extend living spaces or improve rooftop circulation. They tested it on the rooftop of an Afghan family’s cramped rental home. The landlord had locked something suspicious in a small Iranit shed, roof and walls were sealed with the same material, making the space stifling and dark. The students proposed a wooden staircase for rooftop access and redesigned the ceiling with a lighter, bricolaged wooden system to replace the heavy Iranit. They even built part of the ceiling as a mock-up.

## Neighborhood of Things
In Khazaneh’s small homes, possessions often exceeded the capacity of the architecture. Objects spilled into every available space, disregarding walls, doors, and intended uses. Kitchens migrated into courtyards; sofas claimed alleyways; rooms meant for public functions became bedrooms. Nearly every home housed a motorcycle indoors—slipping through the narrow dead ends with fluidity, in sharp contrast to the fixed path of the train.

This led a student to wonder: could a neighborhood be designed without architectural elements, organized entirely by objects? In her imagined scenario, the flowing, continuous motion of the motorcycle became the guiding logic.

In a dead-end of eight houses she proposed a fictional experiment: what if residents pooled their belongings to create shared access to amenities? When she asked, six households agreed. One of the two who declined offered only their courtyard; the rest of their house remained private. 

Based on these permissions, walls of the willing homes were removed. Instead of enclosed rooms, she defined activity zones entirely through objects: a cooking area marked by stoves and utensils; a washing zone with machines and clotheslines. Outdoors, these clusters took shape from shared items. Enclosed spaces were improvised, walls made from wardrobes, roofs from beds, with sleeping areas on top. Above them, makeshift columns of fans and air conditioners were draped with fabric, forming shaded areas for communal life: watching TV, eating, studying. Bathing spaces were divided by gender, and toilets tucked discreetly into corners. The motorcycle threaded between zones, binding the neighborhood of things in motion.

## Final Notes
Marginalized neighborhoods on Tehran’s south side reveal fundamental questions for architecture under scarcity. Should architecture restore order, fix, categorize, and create coherence when facing apparent “problems”? If so, will interventions be accepted by the context? Or is the role of the architect instead to discover overlooked resources, learn from the ingenuity of incompleteness (nakameli), and act without overwriting existing practices?

These neighborhoods bear the marks of rapid, top-down expansion, which often erases spontaneous, place-based practices that transform limited resources into everyday solutions. Rooftops threaded together, mundane techniques of ordinary craftspeople, daily objects that shape everyday life, and other modest practices demonstrate ingenuity that modernist discourse often dismisses as non-standard or overly complex. Yet these practices persist, not because they conform to external standards, but because they actually work, resilient to top-down narratives.

In this essay I have gone through my personal experience with Studio Nakameli as an experimental space for architectural education, which reflects on how architecture can encounter marginalized practices. Rather than summarizing the studio, I recount moments, fragments, and reflections that together reveal how it thinks and acts.

Perhaps it is not standard for all design studios to end with the construction of mock-ups. In ours, students work at 1:1 scale—not out of mere technical concern, but because thinking evolves through making. In premodern, place-based building traditions, thought, craft, and material context were inseparable; each shaped the others in real time. Contemporary practice often fractures this entanglement: design is completed before materials are touched, and making is outsourced or industrialized. In contrast, our students experienced a rhythm in which thinking emerged through engagement with materials, constraints, and context.

Here, “material” is never only physical. It carries social, political, and symbolic weight. It resists, surprises, proposes. In such an exchange, architecture becomes less an act of execution and more a conversation—in which materials, contexts, and people all hold agency, shaping and being shaped in return.
