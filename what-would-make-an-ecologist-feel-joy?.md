# What would make an ecologist feel joy?

**Architectural essay by Fereshteh Assadzadeh Sheikhjani and Mohamadreza Ghodousi, 2026**

The essay appeared in the *Experimental Labs: Grafting Al-Asmakh* booklet as an attachment to [*Al Journal Vol. 2*](https://qm.org.qa/en/about-us/publications/modern-art/al-journal-volume-one/) by Qatar Museums. *Experimental Labs: Grafting Asmakh* was an international workshop organized by Qatar Museums in Doha in 2026, bringing together architects, designers, artists, ecologists and researchers to investigate the historic district of Al Asmakh and its relationship to the rapidly transforming urban landscape of Doha.

> *A walk through Doha reveals a city of stages and backstages—polished enclaves performing national identity alongside vast, mute zones of generic fabric. In the historic district of Asmakh, a demolished building leaves behind an unfinished plot where wild plants seed themselves unprompted. The essay asks what forms of life might emerge from minimal maintenance, and whether bottom-up observation might matter more than top-down intervention.*

One day of the workshop was spent with Dr Aspa (Aspasia Chatziefthimiou), an ecologist. Throughout the day she returned to a single question: *what would make an ecologist feel joy?*

Toward the afternoon she led us off the main paths of Al Bidda Park to a neglected corner beyond the designed promenades. There, layers of introduced vegetation had slipped out of their manicured arrangement. In the absence of continuous maintenance, a different order had begun to emerge: autonomous, entangled and slightly unruly. It was here, behind the controlled landscape, that she suggested we might find an answer.

## Doha: Layered Stages and Backstages

<br>

![doha-from-plane](https://github.com/fereshtehsdz/website-post-test/blob/ecologist-images/ecologist_images_03.jpg)

<br>

At first glance, Doha presents itself as flawless. In Msheireb, new and restored buildings are sutured into a coherent urban image. Fragments of vernacular dwelling practices are folded into a modern, walkable and carefully landscaped district. Unlike other commercial enclaves in the city, Msheireb avoids the overt atmosphere of a theme park. Yet small details complicate the picture.

Escalators eliminate physical friction. High-end hotels and retail establish subtle social thresholds. Museums deliver complete narratives of heritage and nationhood. The district feels composed for legibility. Everything appears calibrated to be seen, interpreted and consumed correctly.

This Doha performs a story: modern progress aligned with an intentional national identity. Such performance requires constant technical and managerial support. Even the tram circulating through the district begins to feel less like infrastructure and more like a reassuring image of public transport.

During the workshop we moved from one iconic enclave to another, each staging a specific narrative. The city revealed itself as a sequence of carefully framed scenes.

One Friday night I chose to walk from the hotel towards the city centre. A few steps beyond the entrance, the seamless image dissolved. The forty-minute walk grew increasingly heavy. There was little of the spontaneity one associates with urban life. I passed through neighbourhoods largely occupied by men gathered in subdued clusters after a week of labour. Crossing vast arterial roads was difficult yet unavoidable. Large building objects–completed or stalled–stood mass-produced and mute.

If the polished Doha is a curated representation, this other condition is its operational counterpart. It sustains the performance while remaining excluded from it.

The city thus oscillates between stage and backstage. In another context such polarity might generate productive tension. Here, however, the two remain segregated. What emerges is not an urban field but a duality of urban objects: iconic, exceptional architectures on one side; generic, repetitive fabric on the other. Unlike Tehran, where socio-spatial differences unfold along a continuous north–south spectrum, Doha arranges its extremes in adjacent yet disconnected striped layers. Highways function as infrastructural voids, preventing friction and, with it, transformation.

## Asmakh: Tension Between Stage and Backstage
These extremes formed a diagnostic lens through which Asmakh could be examined.

Historic fabric, by definition, belongs to the backstage. It is spatially inefficient for contemporary densities; its streets cannot accommodate modern traffic; its infrastructure is outdated. Yet it also contains sedimented forms of collective life, spatial traces that sustain historical continuity.

Preservation, however, can become procedural. Protection risks being reduced to compliance, a checklist of formal gestures. In Asmakh, past tectonics are reconstructed at the level of appearance, while contemporary systems are discreetly concealed behind them. The result is not adaptive continuity but aesthetic stabilisation. Space is rendered safe from unpredictability.

In safeguarding roots, the friction and imperfection that once animated them are removed. What remains is a mummified urban fragment: historically referential and visually coherent, yet detached from the dynamic processes that produced it. The backstage is converted into a stage, performing a historical narrative through over-determination.

## Bottom-up Observation, Before Bottom-up Intervention

<br>

![asmakh-empty-plot](https://github.com/fereshtehsdz/website-post-test/blob/ecologist-images/ecologist_images_04.jpg)
###### Empty plot with wild plants in Asmakh
<br><br>

In Asmakh, still partly a construction site, we encountered an empty plot left by the demolition of a concrete structure incompatible with the historic image. The ground remained unfinished. No standardised paving had yet sealed it. Wild plants punctured the sandy surface.

Soil stores memory in the form of seeds. A brief rain is enough to activate them. The incompleteness of the site had allowed a different possibility to surface: life emerging without authorisation.

If these self-seeded plants were understood as cultural practices embedded in society, what forms might grow from them under minimal maintenance? What latent capacities remain before a context is fully paved over?

A one-week encounter is insufficient to identify such seeds. Yet it can indicate a methodological shift: bottom-up observation before intervention; close reading before design. Historically grounded, sociological attention to existing practices may reveal forms of agency that are both rooted and emergent.

Contemporary development in Qatar is often framed as maturation. At a deeper level, however, it risks displacing seeds already present in the soil–most visibly through economic optimisation and global positioning. Alongside these processes operates a subtler cultural mode: the adoption of imported ideals within a fundamentally different social and historical terrain.

Introspection conducted from the outside risks becoming a vertical gaze, a form of self-orientalisation. What already exists is aestheticised as heritage or dismissed as obsolete. Pre-existing practices are rendered decorative at best and inefficient at worst. The naming of pre-Islamic Arabia as Jahiliyyah offers a historical reminder of how legitimacy can be withdrawn through narrative framing.

To observe from below–in a participatory, socio-historical manner–is to recognise that what appears trivial or outdated may contain generative capacity, without requiring large-scale intervention.
